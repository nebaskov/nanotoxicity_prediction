Using magnetic nanoparticles in MRI and hyperthermia treatment

Medical treatments using magnetic nanoparticles have already shown prospective results in clinic practice. As an example, Feridex® and Resovist® have been approved as magnetic resonance imagining (MRI) contrast agents for liver imagining; several other magnetic nanoparticles for MRI have undergone various stages of clinical trials. The reason for this is using magnetic nanoparticles as MRI contrast agents detailed images of different tissues can be obtained that makes diagnosis of lesions more accurate. Moreover, magnetic nanoparticles have recently been used in medical trials for hyperthermia treatment glioblastoma, prostate and pancreatic cancer. It is possible because in this process cells undergo heat stress so apoptosis, signal transduction and protein expression can be controlled. Using magnetic nanoparticles in biomedical applications is possible thanks to the fact that performance of magnetic nanoparticles in biomedical applications can be controlled adjusting size, shape, surface, composition of nanoparticles and parameters of applied electromagnetic field.
Detailed information can be found in the relevant review articles on the application of magnetic nanoparticles in MRI and hyperthermia.




Descriptors

Dataset was formed during the data processing that includes implementation of new descriptors using initial parameters, correlation matrix analysis, removing outliers and filling of missing data.
SAR and r1, r2 relaxivities were chosen as predictive parameters because they reflect performance of nanoparticles in hyperthermia treatment or MRI. They depend on many factors, so essential parameters were extracted from articles and using them some derivative descriptors were calculated. All descriptors in the database reflect the composition of core as average magnetic moment of core metals; composition of surface as spin of surface atoms, number of H acceptors and LogP for organic coating molecules; shape as maximum to minimum length ratio; size and surface fraction as surface area volume ratio; magnetic properties of nanoparticle as coercivity, remanent and saturation magnetizations; experimental conditions as field strength, amplitude and frequency, concentration of nanoparticles.






Prediction algorithms

For SAR prediction LightGBM Regressor was used with R2 = 0.86, RMSE = 0.28 in 10-fold cross validation and R2 = 0.86, RMSE = 0.26 on test samples; ExtraTrees Regressor showed R2 = 0.72, RMSE = 0.30 in 10-fold cross validation and R2 = 0.78, RMSE = 0.27 on test samples for prediction r1 relaxivity and this algorithm also is the best for prediction r2 relaxivity values – it shows R2 = 0.71 and RMSE = 0.25 in 10-fold cross-validation and R2 = 0.75 and RMSE = 0.22 for test sample




Recourse functions

— Database of magnetic nanoparticles for r1/r2 relaxivities and SAR with samples mined from scientific articles and their visualizations
— The opportunity to offer your sample
— Prediction algorithms for different levels of user request:
– The basic level (MRI and hyperthermia): user enters only the composition of core and size of nanoparticles. When predicting it is considered that the nanoparticle is spherical and uncoated
– The progressive level (MRI and hyperthermia) suggests describing nanoparticle: its core and shell composition, size and shape as well as experimental conditions, except of magnetic properties of nanoparticles such as Saturation and Remanent Magnetization, Coercivity
– Advanced level (MRI and hyperthermia) assumes entering all descriptors: 11 for SAR and 8 for relaxivities prediction



Prediction limitations

It is important to underline the current limitations of ML models to define robustness f predictions using new samples and to understand what data is needed to improve the model performance. First of all, most of the data in the database are ferrites with different compositions, so predictions properties of other types of materials may be more inaccurate. The prevalent distribution of parameters in the database is non-Gaussian, so algorithms predict biomedical characteristics of nanomaterials with high accuracy in areas of the highest probability density of descriptors (see Data Analysis in Methods). Moreover, descriptors used in this work do not describe all aspects of a given system. For example, because of lack of data such descriptors as hydrodynamic radius or zeta-potential were not used so behavior of nanoparticles in the environment was not taken into account. Shell thickness is an important parameter that influences the properties of magnetic nanoparticles, however because of few articles with measurements of this value, this descriptor was not used. Worth saying that all data used by ML models was collected from in vitro measurements, so these models do not reflect behavior of magnetic nanoparticles in vivo at all. For prediction of values in more complex biological systems, a large quantity of descriptors taking into account the huge number of interactions in the body must be used. Thus, there is a need for a thorough characterization of experiments in scientific articles for better quality of data and development of more complex descriptors for ML models.
